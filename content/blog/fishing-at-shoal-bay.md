Port Stephens is renowned as one of the best recreational fishing destinations in New South Wales. The estuary, the ocean, and the national park beaches offer very different experiences — here's how to find your spot.

## Fishing from the Shore

**Shoal Bay Beach** is a good spot for whiting, bream, and flathead, particularly in the early morning and late afternoon. A simple running sinker rig with prawns or worms works well in the calm water.

**The rocks at Tomaree Head** are more adventurous and require care — but they produce good fish, including tailor, luderick, and the occasional jewfish (mulloway). Go with an experienced rock fisherman the first time and always check the swell before standing on any rock platform.

**Fly Point** on the southern side of the Shoal Bay headland is a popular spot for squid jigging and light tackle fishing from the rocky foreshore.

## Fishing from a Boat

The estuary offers bream, flathead, and whiting around the seagrass beds and sand flats. The mouth of the port is good for kingfish and pelagics on lures. The offshore reefs produce snapper, pearl perch, and amberjack.

Several charter operators run half-day and full-day fishing charters from Nelson Bay Marina.

## Hire and Tackle

**Salamander Bay** has a good fishing and tackle shop for bait, rigs, and advice. The staff are knowledgeable and happy to tell you what's running at the moment.

You can also hire kayaks locally — kayak fishing in the shallower parts of the estuary is a great way to spend a morning.

## What You'll Need

All recreational fishers in NSW need a **Recreational Fishing Licence** (available online from the NSW DPI website). Rules on bag limits and minimum sizes are strictly enforced — check before you go.

Minimum recommended gear: a 3–4kg spin rod, a 3000 series reel loaded with 10lb braid, and a few running sinker rigs. Bring plenty of bait — frozen prawns and fresh worms are reliable.

## Best Time to Fish

The golden rule is simple: fish the tide changes. The hour either side of the tide turning is usually the most productive, regardless of location. The bite also tends to be better in low light — early morning and evening.
